How One Well Transformed a Kenyan Village's Future

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Article
Sarah Mitchell
Field Correspondent
June 12, 2026 6 min read Clean Water

When the first water pump was turned on in Kibera Village, nobody expected to see grown men cry with joy. But water — clean and flowing and free — meant something different here than anywhere else in the world. It meant freedom.

For decades, the women of Kibera had risen before dawn to walk a four-hour round trip to the nearest river. The water they carried back was often contaminated. Their children grew sick. Their daughters missed school to help carry the jerry cans. The village had adapted to scarcity so completely that many residents had stopped imagining anything else was possible.

"Before the well, we were survivors. Now we can be builders." — Maria, Village Elder, Kibera

The HopeWave Water Initiative

In early 2025, HopeWave partnered with local engineers and community leaders to drill and install a solar-powered borehole and purification system in Kibera. The project, funded by 1,240 donors from 32 countries, took eight months from groundbreaking to first water.

The system uses UV filtration to remove pathogens without chemicals, powered entirely by a solar array that also charges community lighting. It requires minimal maintenance and can serve 500 families with clean water 365 days a year.

What Changed

  • School attendance for girls in Kibera rose by 38% in the first six months
  • Reported waterborne illnesses dropped by over 60% at the local clinic
  • Three women started small kitchen gardens using reclaimed grey water
  • The village council allocated saved water-walking time to a new literacy program

These numbers are meaningful. But they don't capture the afternoon I spent sitting with Amina, a 12-year-old who is now in school for the first time, learning to read. She told me she wants to be an engineer. She wants to build water systems for other villages.

What Comes Next

Kibera is one of twelve villages in our current Clean Water phase. With continued donor support, we aim to complete all twelve by the end of 2026 — bringing clean water access to over 30,000 people. The cost per beneficiary works out to less than $20. That's less than most of us spend on coffee in a week.

If you'd like to help fund the next well, visit our Causes page and support the Clean Water Initiative. Every dollar — and every share — matters more than you know.
